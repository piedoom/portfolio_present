The first I'd like to show is a passion project of mine called Resamplr, which is a web company marketplace for resources relating to digital music production.

Light Background

Musicians can add real instruments digitally through a process called "sampling". Resamplr is an online marketplace for sampled instruments.

This is a project I've been working on for over 4 years. I'll lightly touch upon earlier instances but will focus mainly on final iterations and process work.

So, before I get into more detail, here's a little bit of background on what Resamplr is and what technology it uses.

Resamplr is a webstore for sampled instruments.
Sampling is a process where a real-life instrument are digitized for use in a virtual studio. (For instance, game or movie studios often use a purely sampled orchestra instead of an expensive live performance - and hobbyist music producers add sampeled instruments to add realism to their tracks.)

Sampled instruments are being used more and more, with flagship audio companies like Native Instruments offering huge instrument libraries for thousands of dollars.

As a musician myself, I've created several sampled instruments, and I wanted a way to publish them for others to use.

Just in terms of this presentation's scope, Resamplr has a ton of versions and process work, but today I'll just be highlighting more recent iterations and what I've learned.

The Problem

Companies that create digital instruments often implement poor & impersonal user experiences.

The Solution

Create a modern and approachable audio software company that puts users' needs
first. Sales follow customer happiness.

I identified multiple problems with current solutions relating to both audio companies and web stores in general.

- Audio companies are still very proprietary, closed, and impersonal. Their web presence is usually sterile and very corporate - which doesn't really mesh with music culture.

- I also sought to create this site because current solutions weren't specialized enough, and took too much profit. One-off marketplace sites like Sellfy didn't allow for much branding work, and took as much as 20%.

----

- the solution was to create my own approachable audio software marketplace that had a focus on user-centric design.

- Additionally, by creating my own site I could integrate another payment processor and bring fees down to as little as 3%.

User Journey

A digital commerce site isn't exactly groundbreaking territory - but it's easy to get things wrong.

My goal is to create as little friction as possible and encourage the user to purchase items.

I started by creating a user journey with a simple chart illustrating user navigation.

While a commerce site doesn't seem particularly exciting or groundbreaking, there are a lot of common pitfalls I see in other commerce sites.

User Journey - Login

User sign-in pages can be where many potential customers lose interest.

Let's have as little friction as possible.

For instance, many sites will require a login before an item can be added to a user cart. Asking a user to login creates unnecessary friction that may cause them to leave.

Instead, I designed the site to get users invested before requiring a login or credit card number. Any user - logged in or not - can wishlist items. That small time investment will encourage them to go the extra step to create an account or enter in payment information later.

On the product side of the user experience, I made sure to promote free resources and sound packs to encourage new users to register an account.

Issues & Improvements

I created this design for Resamplr in 2016. However, I went back and reviewed my design in early 2018 as I learned a lot since my first iteration.

This next section will highlight the problems I found with my previous design, and what I did to resolve those issues.
Taking a look at the homepage, there is a lot that can be improved.
The very thin white text on a saturated, bright red background has very poor contrast and readability.

This problem is even more pronounced when light text is over an image.
Additionally, while I may have written a summary of what Resamplr *is*, it's better to *show* what services it provides. Supporting text is great, but for the most part users skip text and go straight to media and other content!
Moving on to the login area -
- it still has some contrast issues. Also, it doesn't have a forgot password button!
By far, I learned the most from the product page.
The usage of color here was somewhat inconsistent and led to poor visual hierarchy.
Additionally some controls were also duplicated across the page which could confuse users.
Finally, the visual aesthetic - while clean - lacked the vibrancy and soul of what a music website should display.

New Iterations

In early 2018, I reworked my previous design and focused on fixing the issues I mentioned above.
The new homepage shows users what Resamplr provides, instead of telling them with boring text. Products are shown on the first page, along with a playable sample, description, and price. The bottom of the page features an area where social content could be displayed from tutorials to giveaways.

The top of the page presents a huge banner that shows featured products. I had something similar with my older design, except the link redirected to the products page. After looking at clicks using google analytics, I noticed many users clicked through but then left. I hoped that linking directly to a product would help user engagment.

In terms of visual style, I used complementary colors as well as angles to make the page more exciting and dynamic and I drew inspiration from jazz-era posters and media.
The typography is still modern and thin, but much more legible. A slightly thicker font combined with a dark-on-light theme greatly helps visibility.

The login form provides all features users would expect.
The support page was clearly labeled and answered as many frequent questions as possible. A contact form is at the bottom as a last resort to encourage users to take a look at the FAQ first to see if their question has already been answered.
On to the product page -
I believe the biggest improvement was a smarter usage of color. Purple was reserved for interactions and other high-importance sections.

The new one-column layout helped cut down on visual noise and better guide the user towards content and media.
Additionally, the product header allowed for a customizable background image and logo. This made individual product branding possible.

Animate

The second project I'd like to show today is a desktop audio application called Animate.

Animate is an audio effect filter, which means it takes input audio from vocals or an instrument and changes it digitally (similar to a guitar pedal)

The Problem

There are currently no applications that allow for creative filter design. Those applications are difficult to animate or change over time.

The Solution

Create a flexible and intuitive audio filter automation program by utilizing affordances learned through other programs.

There's a lot of audio effects on the internet, so why make another? Well, no current solutions allow for the effect to easily modulate over time. Additionally, most modern audio effects look like theyre stuck in 2001 with confusing and skeuomorphic designs.

My solution was to create an all-in-one filter design plugin with animation tools built in, as well as to create a consistent design language for application controls.

Examples

Because this product is somewhat domain-specific, I have a couple examples set up to better illustrate the project's uses and goals.

Low Pass

Filters out all high-pitched frequencies

I loaded up a generic filter plugin to demonstrate the connection between visuals and audio.

I fed static noise into the filter and created a low-pass, which cut all higher frequencies.

High Pass

Filters out all low-pitched frequencies

A high-pass on the other hand, filters out all lower frequencies.

Goal

Automate filters easily.

My goal was to let a user to mix and match filters and then animate between them easily. Those filters could create everything from dubstep wobble basses to cool sci-fi sound designs.

Current solutions

  • Too simplistic
  • Not many customization options
  • Poor automation support
  • Skeuomorphic UI
  • Bad UX that requires a manual
As I noted before, other filters exist, but don't have great user experience, and don't allow for easy keyframing and animation.

Firstly, the app can only occupy one window. I also avoid tabs as they usually end up being clunky.

Secondly, the application has to be compact. Users will have multiple plugins on their screen at once, so it has to find the right balance of information density.

The Reel is a mobile application focused on short sports highlight clips.

Goals

  • Streamlined & Single purpose
  • Adaptive
  • Low friction
  • Content funnel
The Reel is a class project proposed by a former ESPN executive. The idea was to create a simple way for sports fans to watch sports highlight clips.

I kept the project simple & streamlined. Users should be able to easily start using the app.

I wanted the app to adapt to user needs. For instance, a Pittsburgh resident should automatically be recommended Pittsburgh-related teams such as the Steelers.

Finally, I wanted users to spend as much time in the app as possible by offering related videos and topics.

Users

While on transport

Relaxing

Catching up

I began with some general user personas.

User A wants to watch sports highlights on transportation. They want bite-sized content and quick interactions.

User B just wants to relax watching highlights. They want to sit back and watch anything and everything.

User C wants to catch up with the latest so they can talk about sports with their friends and colleagues. They want to follow specific sports and teams.
I began with lofi wireframes.

A simple, optional login lets users quickly get into the app.
A video feed based on user location, possible interests, and later human feedback provide content.
The app has an emphasis on video, with recommendations and social sharing buttons.
Minimalist settings allow for more granular control ---
--- along with the ability to manually add or remove followed topics.

This project had a short timespan, so I wasn't able to include everything I planned in the final comps, but I'd like to show them and explain their visual direction.
Styles are inspired by darker sports-related brands, particularly Nike's line. The general "trend " is composed of darker background colors accented with neon highlights. This app should feel sporty, after all.

Login

While users can sign up with traditional email addresses, signing in with Google or Facebook is also provided. Users can also use the application without signing in to experience the features before saving them to an account.

Users can sign in with the traditional email and password, can also use google or Facebook for easier login. If they wish, they can also bypass the login stage alltogether and save their account later.

Feed

The feed is where the user should be spending 99% of their time. It's a content funnel. Each video is chosen algorithmically based on the user's location, primary language, interests and likes. The goal is to always provide one more video that piques the user's interest.

The feed is where the user should be spending most of their time. It's a constant stream of content chosen algorithmically

based on user location, interests, and likes. The goal is to have the user always thinking "Okay, just one more video..."

More information and related videos are shown upon clicking the video's title, and a graphic showing the related sport is shown on the top right.

Liking a Video

In order to provide a more granular control over shown content, the user can "like " a video. That video will be saved to their account to watch or share later. It will also help guide the home feed algorithm to display fitting content.

Users can like a video to save it to their favorites. Additionally, those likes will help the underlying search engine find them more videos and sports related to their interests.

Following Topics

While anticipated automatically, users can manage their interests. They can search for specific sports, athletes, and networks to follow. Users can also unfollow suggested topics in this section.

While topics are automatically anticipated by an underlying algorithm, users can have finer control over their followed interests in the topics section. Here, they can search for new topics manually, or unfollow ones they aren't interested in.
