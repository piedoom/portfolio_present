Light Background

Musicians can add real instruments digitally through a process called "sampling". Resamplr is an online marketplace for sampled instruments.

This is a project I've been working on for over 4 years. I'll lightly touch upon earlier instances but will focus mainly on final iterations and process work.

So, before I get into more detail, here's a little bit of background on what Resamplr is and what technology it uses.

Resamplr is a webstore for sampled instruments.
Sampling is a process where a real-life instrument are digitized for use in a virtual studio. (For instance, piano sounds in more recent songs are often sampled, digital pianos, instead of real-life pianos.)

Sampled instruments are being used more and more, with flagship audio companies like Native Instruments offering huge libraries for sometimes thousands of dollars.

As a musician myself, I've created several sampled instruments, and I wanted a way to publish them for others to use.

In terms of this presentation's scope, Resamplr has a ton of versions and process work, but today I'll just be highlighting more recent iterations and what I've learned.

The Problem

Companies that create digital instruments often implement poor & impersonal user experiences.

The Solution

Create a modern and approachable audio software company that puts users' needs
first. Sales follow customer happiness.

I identified multiple problems with current solutions relating to both audio companies and web stores in general.

- Audio companies are still very proprietary, closed, and impersonal. Their web presence is usually sterile and very corporate - which doesn't really mesh with music culture.

- I also sought to create this site because current solutions weren't specialized enough, and took too much profit. One-off marketplace sites like Sellfy didn't allow for much branding work, and took as much as 20%.

----

- the solution was to create my own approachable audio software marketplace that had a focus on user-centric design.

- Additionally, by creating my own site I could integrate another payment processor and bring fees down to as little as 3%.

User Journey

A digital commerce site isn't exactly groundbreaking territory - but it's easy to get things wrong.

My goal is to create as little friction as possible and encourage the user to purchase items.

I started by creating a user journey with a simple chart illustrating user navigation.

While a commerce site doesn't seem particularly exciting or groundbreaking, there are a lot of common pitfalls I see in other commerce sites.

User Journey - Login

User sign-in pages can be where many potential customers lose interest.

Let's have as little friction as possible.

For instance, many sites will require a login before an item can be added to a user cart. Asking a user to login creates unnecessary friction that may cause them to leave.

Instead, I designed the site to get users invested before requiring a login or credit card number. Any user - logged in or not - can wishlist items. That small time investment will encourage them to go the extra step to create an account or enter in payment information later.

On the product side of the user experience, I made sure to promote free resources and sound packs to encourage new users to register an account.
After planning out the user journey, I created some quick lofi wireframes to visualize the website flow.

I'll go into greater detail about my process later in the presentation.
I then created a style guide for UI elements. I envisioned something clean, professional, light, and vibrant after drawing inspiration from other prolific music brands like Roland, Yamaha, and Native Instruments.
After creating the style guide, I brought some visual design to my wireframes and created comps.

The title page was meant to introduce new users to what Resamplr is and what it provides.
The login page was clean and quick, with interactions clearly visible.
Audio samples were especially important, so I created several variations before finding a layout that balanced visual design with information.

I planned for the sound player to be persistent through page changes (similar to soundcloud) so the user could browse other products while listening to samples from another product.
The product page shows all relevant information in a summary to the top right; the name, price, and file formats provided were clearly labeled. The middle column provided a more free-form layout.

Issues & Improvements

I created this design for Resamplr in 2016. However, I went back and reviewed my design in early 2018 as I learned a lot since my first iteration.

This next section will highlight the problems I found with my previous design, and what I did to resolve those issues.
Taking a look at the homepage, there is a lot that can be improved.
The very thin white text on a saturated, bright red background has very poor contrast and readability.

This problem is even more pronounced when light text is over an image.
Additionally, while I may have written a summary of what Resamplr *is*, it's better to *show* what services it provides. Supporting text is great, but for the most part users skip text and go straight to media and other content - but there was no product content on my home page!
Moving on to the login area -
- it still suffers from contrast issues. Also, it doesn't have a forgot password button, so users would be locked out forever if they lost their password!
By far, I learned the most from the product page.
The usage of color here was somewhat inconsistent and led to a confusing visual hierarchy.
Additionally some controls were also duplicated across the page which could confuse users.
Finally, the visual aesthetic - while clean - lacked the vibrancy and soul of what a music website should display.

New Iterations

In early 2018, I reworked my previous design and focused on fixing the issues I mentioned above.
The new homepage shows users what Resamplr provides, instead of telling them with boring text. Products are shown on the first page, along with a playable sample, description, and price. The bottom of the page features an area where social content could be displayed from tutorials to giveaways.

I used complementary colors as well as angles to make the page more exciting and dynamic and I drew inspiration from jazz-era posters and media.
The typography is still modern and thin, but much more legible. A slightly thicker font combined with a dark-on-light theme greatly helps visibility.

The login form provides all features users would expect.
The support page was clearly labeled and answered as many frequent questions as possible. A contact form is at the bottom as a last resort to encourage users to take a look at the FAQ first to see if their question has already been answered.
On to the product page -
I believe the biggest improvement was a smarter usage of color. Purple was reserved for interactions and other high-importance sections.

The new one-column layout helped cut down on visual noise and better guide the user towards content and media.
Additionally, the product header allowed for a customizable background image and logo. This made individual product branding possible.

- - - Some simple wireframes. I grouped my wireframe into 3 visual blocks
The first is a visual EQ view, which displays the sound waves being modified as well as different control nodes.

This element is something almost all digital EQs have, so users should be comfortable seeing it there.
Below the visual EQ lies a timeline. The timline controls each node over time, and shows where in time each change happens.

This section is heavily inspired by programs such as After effects, Blender, and other keyframing applications.
To the right is a properties menu. This section allows the user to finely tweak their EQ animation.
In short, each view displayed the information in a different way. The amplitude slider, node position, and keyframe were all connected.
